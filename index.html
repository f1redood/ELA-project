Do you really know how your shirt was made?


Research question:
What are working conditions like in the garment industry?

I picked this research question because I feel it most broadly, but also most accurately, sums up the most important details to know. It is relevant because everyone has contact with the garment industry.




Source 1


Ethical Style: How Is My T-Shirt Made?

Published by GOOD worldwide


Facts
  • 90% of cotton harvested in the US is spun into clothes.
  • The US is one of the largest exporters of cotton.
Relevant information:
  • “...child labor is a major reality in cotton harvesting.”
  • “The only way to meet the bottom line is to shave the last remaining pennies off of the wages of spinners and sewers.”

Conclusion:

The USA is one of the largest importers of cotton T-shirts. However, these shirts may not be ethically made. They might be made with child labor, workers in poor working conditions, or other unethical practices. These might include stifling heat, heavy machinery, unsafe working environments, etc.

Source 2


It's Incredibly Hard To Prove That Clothing Is "Ethically Made"

By Anne D'innocenzio

Published by The New York Times


Facts
  • Less than 1% of all clothes are ethically made.
  • Shoppers seem far more concerned with quality than how the clothes were made.
Relevant information:
  • “...ethically made clothes make up a tiny fraction of 1 percent of the overall $1 trillion global fashion industry.”
  • “...major chains typically use a complex web of suppliers in countries such as Bangladesh, which often contract business to other factories.”

Research notes:

Clothing brands do not know how their clothes are made. The factories they use usually delegate their demand to other factories, which is not tracked by the seller, so they have no bearing of how and where their clothes are made. Because of this, most clothing brands are usually unable to track where their clothes are made. Even if they send the order to a factory with relatively good conditions, the factory might send the order to a factory with worse conditions.

Source 3


An Apparel Factory Defies Sweatshop Label, But Can It Thrive?

By Steven Greenhouse

Published by The New York Times


Facts
  • Most workers are paid as little as they can be.
  • It is rare for workers to be paid fairly.
Relevant information:
  • “Ms. Castillo had long dreamed of a bigger, sturdier house, but three months ago something happened that finally made it possible: she landed a job at one of the world’s most unusual garment factories. Industry experts say it is a pioneer in the developing world because it pays a ‘living wage’ - in this case, three times the average pay of the country’s apparel workers - and allows workers to join a union without a fight.”
  • “The factory is a high-minded experiment, a response to appeals from myriad university officials and students activists that the garment industry stop using poverty-wage sweatshops. It has 120 employees and is owned by Knight Apparel, a privately held company based in Spartanburg, S.C., that is the leading supplier of college-logo apparel to American universities, according to the Collegiate Licensing Company.”

Research notes:

A factory for Knights Apparel is trying to give employees a better working experience by giving them living wages and allowing them to join unions. This has led to people who work at the factory being able to move up in society instead of being stuck at the bottom. This has even led Ms. Castillo, as mentioned in the article, to be able purchase a new home. This is but one example of ways this new development has positively impacted someone's life.

Conclusion


All in all, I concluded that it is a rare thing for employees to be treated fairly in clothing factories. Throughout my research, I have heard many stories of factory workers working in terrible conditions such as being forced to use heavy machinery or toxic chemicals, and many stories of these workers being paid a pittance for their toil. Therefore, I believe that working conditions in the garment industry are generally very bad. For instance, child labor is often used in clothing factories, and the children who work in these factories often use unsafe, heavy machinery and are paid very little for theirhard work.

I would advise consumers to try to check if their clothing brands have been accused of using unfair labor in order to determine if they shoud be bought from. Even one person boycotting a clothing brand can make a difference.
